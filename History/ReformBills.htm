SECOND AND THIRD REFORM BILLS

Queen Victoria's reign saw an alternation of great Prime Ministers, some belonging to the Tories, now called conservatives (Peel, Disraeli), some to the Wings, now re-christened Liberals (Palmerston, Gladstone). They had different views of current problems and, obviously, offered different solutions.
The Liberals, for instance, proposed to extend the franchise and, in 1867, the Second Reform Bill was passed (though under a Conservative Government), which gave the right of vote the town labourers, but left the agricultural labourers and miners still unfranchised.
It was only in 1884, with the Third Reform Bill,that the latter got that vote. The electorate of England was thus doubled, suffrage being extended to all male workers. Now many representatives of the working class could enter the House of Commons.
