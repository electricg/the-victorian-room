THE WORKHOUSE

Workhouses were institutions for paupers. Workhouses were supported by public funds, or by parish, or "union" funds. In workhouses paupers were fed and lodged and set to work. This expression of public charity, though, was hardly better than prison: it was the last step before starving. Dirty bleak places inhabited by the areas of society, workhouses were the dustbin of Victorian society.

Why did people enter the Workhouse?

People usually lived there because they were too poor, old or ill to support themselves, because they lost their work during periods of high unemployment and couldn't provide care for themselves when they became elderly or sick. Unmarried pregnant women were often disowned by their families and the workhouse was the only place they could go during and after the birth of their child. Workhouses, though, were never prisons, and entry into them was generally a voluntary although often painful decision.

Inside the workhouse

The workhouse was like a small self-contained village. Apart from the basic rooms such as a dining-hall for eating, and dormitories for sleeping the workhouses often had their own bakery, laundry, tailor's and shoe-maker's, vegetable gardens and orchards, and even a piggery for rearing pigs. There would also be school-room, nurseries, fever-wards for the sick, a chapel, and a dead-room or mortuary. Once inside the workhouse, inmate's only possessions were their uniform and the bed they had in the large dormitory. Beds were simply constructed with a wooden or iron frame, and could be as little as two feet across. Bed-sharing was also very common.

The daily routine

The daily routine for inmates proposed by the Poor Law Commissioners was as follows:

Rules and Regulations

There were strict rules inside the workhouses. These were often printed and prominently displayed in the workhouse, and also read out aloud each week so that the illiterate could have no excuse for disobeying them.

Workhouse Uniform

Workhouse clothing could be either bought-in or else made by workhouse inmates themselves as a work task. It was usually made from fairly coarse materials with the emphasis being on hard-wearing rather than on comfort and fitting.

Diet

The diet to workhouse in mates was particularly detailed. For example:
  • 7 Ounces of Meat when dressed, without Bones, to every grown Person
  • 2 Ounces of Butter
  • 4 Ounces of Cheese
  • 1 Pound of Bread
  • 3 Pints of Beer
From 1835 onwards, the Poor Law Commissioners issued sample dietary tables for use in Union workhouse.
Children and the aged or infirm had a slightly different diet, usually with more meat-based meals, and with inclusion of milk or tea. The main constituent of the workhouse diet was bread. At breakfast it was supplemented by gruel or porridge. Workhouse broth was usually the water used for boiling the dinner meat. Tea, often without milk, was often provided for the aged and infirm at breakfast, together with a small amount of butter. Supper was usually similar to breakfast.
Meals were usually eaten, in silence, in a large dining-hall which often doubled-up as a chapel.
The inmates commonly sat in rows all facing the same way, with men men and women separated.


Work

Inmates were given a variety of work to perform, much of which was involved in running the workhouse. The women mostly did domestic jobs such as cleaning, or helping in the kitchen or laundry. Some workhouses had workshop for sewing, spinning and weaving or other local trades. Others had their own vegetable gardens where the inmates worked to provide food for the workhouse. In rural areas, inmates were sometimes used for agriculture labour. Stone-breaking was a task often given to male inmates.
By 1930, workhouses were officially abolished.
