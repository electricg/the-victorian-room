THE POLICE

The 18th century had been a rough and disorderly age, with mob violence, violent crimes, highwaymen, smugglers and the new temptations to disorder brought about by the Industrial Revolution. In 1829 the Metropolitan Police Force, organized by Sir Robert Peel, was established to keep the order in London. The force was essentially a civilian one: its members were armed only with wooden truncheons and at first wore top-hats and blue frock-coats. Their primary purpose was to prevent crime, and some London criminals left their haunting grounds of London for the larger provincial towns, which in turn established their own forces on the Metropolitan model. The pattern followed through to the small villages and countryside. At the turn of the century, the British police force established a reputation for humane and kindly efficiency. Their mere existence undoubtedly did a lot to prevent crime, and they built up what was on the whole a highly effective system of investigation and and arrest.
