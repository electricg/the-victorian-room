DIET

Until 1870 only a quarter of the food eaten in Britain came from abroad. The food eaten by most people was limited to what could be grown or reared here. Meat was expensive and in these conditions it would have been eaten only rarely; fish was cheaper, but for many people their main food was bread and margarine or bread and dripping. Carrots, cabbage, potatoes and onions were the principal vegetables available. Fruit was a luxury. The development of railways made fresh food more widely available and the faster sea-travel allowed by steamship brought exotic products from abroad, extending the season for perishables. The USA, Canada, Argentina, Australia and New Zealand began to flood European markets with cheaper meat and especially grain, bringing the price of bread down. The invention of refrigeration enabled more fresh meat and dairy produce to be available all year round; canning was also being developed to allow preservation or fresh food.
