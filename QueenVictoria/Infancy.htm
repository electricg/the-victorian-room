HER INFANCY

The symptoms of the terrible illness of King George III confined him in Windsor Castle while his eldest son, George, Prince of Wales, ruled for him. His brother; Victoria's father, Edward, Duke of Kent, was the fourth son and as his brothers didn't have any heir, it seemed that this could be a great occasion for him to became one day King. He married at the age of 50 only to give a successor to the throne.
Edward knew Victoire, Victoria's mother, thanks to the Princess Charlotte, daughter of the Prince of Wales, married to Leopold of Saxe-Coburg-Saalfeld, that suggested to the uncle that he would find an amiable wife in Leopold's sister.
Victoire was 31 years old when she married Edward and the marriage seemed to her a great occasion to change her life, from the little German village to the one of the great Court of Europe.
In 1819 Victoria was born and her father was not disappointed that the baby was not a male. She was named "Alexandrina Victoria", because her chief godfather was the Russian Tsar Alexander, but she was known simply as Victoria.
After the death of the Duke of Kent, Victoire decided to stay in London with her brother, because her little daughter was third in line to the throne.
She passed her infancy with kind people: her mother, always worried for the child's health, her half-sister Feodora, a constant companion, the nurse Mrs. Brock, replaced by Feodora's governess and numerous aunts and uncles of the Royal family. Her character was difficult, she was naughty, screaming and stamping. But after these fits of temper she was also bright enough, quick at lessons and shrew in personal relationship: when her uncle George IV asked her which song she wished to listen to, she requested "God Save the King".
In 1831 Leopold went away from England permanently to take up the crown of the newly-created kingdom of Belgium. Left alone and used to be ruled by men, she was strictly guarded by Conroy. He had been the Duke of Kent's aid-de-camp, a military man and a good-looking Irishman, with charm and ambition.It was Conroy and the Duchess of Kent who insisted that Victoria should be strictly guarded and controlled for the fear of a fatal "accident" by the hands of the Duke of Cumberland. And it was Conroy too who find for Feodora a husband when it seemed that the widower King could marry her and deprive Victoria a chance to become Queen. His only aim was to be the chief advisor of the future Queen of England.After the death of King George Victoria's education became more controlled and rigid and only thanks to Lehzen the princess understood the significance of her place in the royal family. And it was Baroness Lehzen too that opened the Princess's eyes to Conroy's intentions. On 24th May 1837 she celebrated her 18th birthday: from this moment she was free from the possibility of a regency and closer to the throne.At 6 o'clock on the morning of 20th June the Duchess of Kent woke her daughter with the news that William IV was dead. So Victoria received, alone for the first time, the Archibishop of Canterbury and Lord Chamberlain. All the accounts were paid: Lehzen became her closest confident, her mother was left at home and to Conroy was forbidden the access to her private room. Only after many years the Duchess of Kent regained her daughter's affection.
