NEO-GOTHIC

The term "Gothic Revival" (sometimes called Victorian Gothic) usually refers to the period of mock-Gothic architecture practised in the second half of the 19th century. According to Pugin, one of the most influential Victorian architects, Classical architecture was foreign and pagan, whereas Gothic was English and Christian. While the Classical style might be acceptable in towns and cities, it should never be seen in the English countryside. Although the beginning of the Arts and Crafts movement is often attributed to the building of William Morris' Red House in 1859, the true birth of the movement happened some 25 years earlier when Pugin publicly railed against a newly industrialized society that was increasingly separating designer from laborer. During the late 1840's, Ruskin, an art history professor at Oxford University, began a campaign to return in England to a simpler way of life in tune with nature.
His vision called for the elimination of machine-made decoration and clean design free from foreign influence. The English, having borrowed, heavily from the French in order to furnish their Victorian lifestyle, soon began to cast its collective eye inward for inspiration and there followed a revival of English Gothic and Medieval styles. The fashion of Gothic Revival was a curious mixture of English Gothic with French, Venetian and Italian version. Symmetry was deliberately avoided. The problem for High Victorian design was how to reconcile strength with richness, the answer was to ornament construction and construct ornament.
