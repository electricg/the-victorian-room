A VICTORIAN HOUSE


The first room you come upon after entering the foyer is the dining room. Early in the era, this dining room was located in the basement with the kitchen. It was once believed food digested better in the dark. Later, the room was moved to the first floor. You’ll notice the furniture is a dark wood and very ornate in style. The table is open fully to accommodate guests. You will usually find a carved "sideboard" (china closet) and buffet. There is also a dinner wagon which is used to wheel food and drinks to the table from the dumbwaiter.

Like most Victorian kitchens, this is located in the basement to keep the rest of the house free from odors and grease. Here you will find one large table and several smaller ones which are used to chop food, roll dough and place dry dishes on. In addition, to the stove, you will find a fireplace which is also used for cooking.
The dishes and groceries are stored in wooden cabinets with glass doors. The pies are kept in a small cabinet called a "pie safe".
In the scullery would be a copper, sink, draining board and a mangle.

The parlor, the most formal room in the house, is used only on Sundays for family gatherings and entertaining guests. Most parlors are decorated in deep, rich colors of burgundy or dark green.
Heavy drapes adorn the windows to prevent the sun from fading the furniture and rugs. People are now beginning to move away from the darker colors to creams and blues. The fireplace is used to keep the room comfortable in winter.
The room’s crowded nature serves two purposes: a. to show the family’s wealth and good taste, and b. to have enough seats for all of one’s guests. It is considered rude for a man to offer his seat to a lady as the cushion might still be warm.

This is one end of the drawing room which is used for many purposes. It is used to entertain when there are large dinner parties. The men retreat here after dinner to smoke their cigars, engage in conversation and play billiards.
At the other end of the room, there’s another seating area. Along one wall is a fairly large bookcase filled with books from ceiling to the floor and a desk. Sitting against another of the walls, there is the piano.
This room is mainly used to read, sew and tend to one’s household and other business matters.

Victorians consider bedrooms or bed chambers, as they are called, very private. They are found on a second or third floor. They are never viewed by guests.
The furnishings, in addition to the bed, wardrobe, bureau and wash stand, often include a chair, table and settee.

One or two rooms might be set aside as a nursery and/or school room.
A large table in the middle of the room would be used for both meals and games.
A high brass fender would keep the children away from the fire.
There would be a screen for use when bathing, a baby bath, cot, and chairs for children and adults.
The floor would be covered with linoleum, with perhaps a rough and hard wearing carpet in a dark grey or brown.

People who have an indoor water closet are quite fortunate. There’s a tub in wood and the toilet is hidden from the general view.
